Research Question: How does increased radiation exposure affect cellular damage in humans during long-duration space missions, and how can material choice mitigate this risk?

Real-World Relevance: Astronauts traveling beyond Earth‚Äôs magnetosphere (e.g., Moon, Mars missions) are exposed to Galactic Cosmic Rays and Solar Particle Events. Effective shielding is essential to reduce cancer risk, DNA damage, and long-term health impacts.

üß¨ Interdisciplinary Approach

Biology

  • Radiation causes DNA strand breaks and oxidative stress.
  • High-LET radiation produces complex, hard-to-repair damage.
  • Reduced dose lowers mutation rates and cancer risk.

Chemistry

  • Atomic number and hydrogen content govern radiation interaction.
  • Hydrogen-rich materials moderate neutrons effectively.
  • Low-Z materials minimize secondary radiation production.

Environmental Science

  • Space environment lacks atmospheric and magnetic shielding.
  • Mission duration increases cumulative radiation dose.
  • Material mass impacts launch cost and sustainability.

üß™ Methodology

This investigation uses a secondary research design supported by computational simulation data from peer-reviewed journals, NASA, ESA, and materials science databases.

  • Compare materials by atomic structure, density, and hydrogen content.
  • Analyse radiation dose reduction per unit mass.
  • Evaluate biological relevance using dose equivalent (Sv).

Planning involved selecting materials commonly proposed for spacecraft shielding and analysing their effectiveness against GCR and SPE radiation.

üìä Results & Data Analysis

Simulation and experimental studies consistently show hydrogen-rich materials outperform aluminium on a mass-for-mass basis.

Key Findings Summary

  • Polyethylene reduces dose equivalent more effectively than aluminium.
  • Metal hydrides provide the highest hydrogen density and dose attenuation.
  • High-Z metals increase secondary radiation despite high density.

‚úÖ Results, Conclusion & Evaluation

  • Increased radiation exposure directly increases cellular DNA damage.
  • Hydrogen-rich materials significantly reduce biologically harmful radiation.
  • Aluminium alone is insufficient due to secondary particle production.

Conclusion: A layered shielding approach using hydrogen-rich materials provides the best protection for astronauts while minimizing mass.
